The old man the boat

If you think that "man" is a modified noun by the adjective "old", you're wrong. The truth is, it acts as a verb meaning: "operate", "work", or "run" some equipment. So you read the sentence like this: "The old [people] man[operate] the boat

Fat people eat accumulates

I can assure that I was as confused as you initially. You see the verb has "-s" at the end, indicating it takes a single noun. Push "people" out of the subject and group it with "eat", then you have: "Fat, people eat, accumulates" [i.e., "The fat that people eat accumulates in their bodies"]

The man whistling tunes pianos

Well, if you pay attention a little better in class, you'd not have revealed the answer so effortlessly. The sentence has already been reduced. Originally, it goes like this: "The man [who whistles] tunes pianos"

The man pushed through the door fell.

No, it's not the man who fell. What it's trying to say is: "The man pushed through the door [and] it [the door] fell"
